16. Imbra

The International Marriage Broker Regulation Act of 2005 (Subtitle D of Title VIII (Sec.831-834) of United States Public Law 109-162), or IMBRA, is a controversial United States federal statute that requires background checks for all marriage visa sponsors and limits serial visa applications. The law also requires background checks for consenting adults who wish to communicate using an international penpal service (even if they have no intention of getting married). The impetus for its introduction was two high-profile cases (including the Susanna Blackwell case in 1995 and the Anastasia King case in 2000) in which foreign women had been abused and eventually murdered by men who had used a K-1 fiancee visa issued by the US State Department to bring them to the United States. In the King case, the husband had physically abused a previous foreign bride before murdering Anastasia. In the Blackwell case, her husband had a clean record, therefore IMBRA would not have prevented her murder. It was intended to stop the abuse of «mail order brides» (a derogatory label) by prospective husbands with criminal histories.
